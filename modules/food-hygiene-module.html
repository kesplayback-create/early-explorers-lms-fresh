Food Safety Law & Your Responsibilities

Every person who handles food is subject to food safety law. Understand the legal framework, what it requires of you, and the principles of HACCP that underpin everything we do.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~6 mins✅ 1 check
Lesson 1 · The Legal Framework

The Law That Governs Food Safety

Food safety in the UK is governed by several pieces of legislation. As a food handler, you are personally responsible for complying with these laws.

Key Legislation
⚖️
Food Safety Act 1990 — makes it an offence to sell food that is unfit, unsafe or not of the nature/substance/quality demanded
📋
Food Hygiene (England) Regulations 2006 — sets hygiene requirements for all food businesses, based on EU Regulation 852/2004
🏛️
Food Standards Agency (FSA) — the government body responsible for food safety policy and enforcement in the UK
🔍
Environmental Health Officers (EHOs) — local authority inspectors who visit food businesses unannounced to check compliance
📌
Ignorance of the law is not a defence. Every food handler must understand and comply with food safety regulations — including you.
🏛️
Ofsted & EHO Both Visit
Early years settings are subject to both Ofsted inspections and unannounced EHO visits. Poor food hygiene ratings directly affect our registration.
5
Star FSA rating — our target
0
Tolerance for food safety breaches
Lesson 1 · HACCP Principles

HACCP — The Food Safety Management System

HACCP stands for Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points. UK law requires all food businesses to have a food safety management system based on HACCP principles. It means identifying risks before they cause harm.

1. Identify hazards
2. Identify critical control points
3. Set critical limits
4. Monitor controls
5. Corrective actions
6. Verify system works
7. Document everything
🌡️
Example CCP: Cooking temperature — the critical limit is 75°C core temperature to destroy pathogens
📋
Your role: Follow documented procedures, complete temperature logs, report problems to the manager immediately
✏️
If you don't record it, it didn't happen — incomplete records are treated as non-compliance during inspections
4 Types of Food Hazard
🦠
Biological — bacteria, viruses, moulds
⚗️
Chemical — cleaning products, pesticides
🔩
Physical — glass, metal, packaging
⚠️
Allergenic — the 14 major allergens

Microbiological Hazards

Bacteria are the most common cause of food poisoning. Learn how they multiply, which pathogens pose the greatest risk, and what conditions allow them to thrive.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~7 mins✅ 1 check
Lesson 2 · How Bacteria Multiply

Binary Fission — Why Speed Matters

Bacteria reproduce by splitting in two — called binary fission. Under ideal conditions, one bacterium can become over one million in just 10 hours. This is why time and temperature control is critical.

⏱️
Bacteria can double every 10–20 minutes in ideal conditions
🌡️
The Danger Zone is 8°C–63°C — bacteria multiply rapidly throughout this range
❄️
Below 8°C — bacteria slow down but do NOT die (fridges slow, freezers halt growth)
🔥
Above 63°C — most bacteria are killed (spores may survive — more on this shortly)
💧
Bacteria need: warmth, moisture, food and time — remove any one factor to slow growth

Lesson 2 · Key Pathogens

Bacteria You Need to Know

These are the food poisoning bacteria most relevant to nursery settings. Each has specific sources and risks for young children, who are in a high-risk group.

SAL
Salmonella — raw poultry, eggs, raw meat
Causes severe vomiting, diarrhoea, fever. Destroyed by thorough cooking. Young children are highly vulnerable.
ECO
E.coli O157 — raw/undercooked beef, unpasteurised milk
Can cause kidney failure in children under 5. Extremely dangerous — very low infectious dose. Cross-contamination is a major risk.
LIS
Listeria — soft cheese, pâté, cooked meats, chilled foods
Grows in the fridge (unlike most bacteria). Particularly dangerous for pregnant women and young children.
CLO
Clostridium perfringens — cooked meat, stews, gravies
Forms heat-resistant spores that survive cooking. Risk when food is cooled slowly or reheated insufficiently.
What Are Spores?
Bacteria's Survival Shield

Some bacteria (like Clostridium and Bacillus cereus) can form spores — a dormant, protective state that survives high temperatures. When food cools, spores germinate and multiply. This is why rapid cooling and correct reheating are critical.

👶
Children under 5 are in the HIGH RISK group — their immune systems cannot fight food poisoning as effectively as adults. Even small amounts of contaminated food can be life-threatening.

Lesson 3 of 7

Contamination & Cross-Contamination

Contamination is one of the most common causes of food poisoning. Learn the four types of contamination, how cross-contamination occurs, and the colour-coded systems that prevent it.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~6 mins✅ 1 check
Lesson 3 · Types of Contamination

Four Ways Food Gets Contaminated

Contamination occurs when a harmful substance enters food. There are four types — and all four must be controlled in a nursery kitchen.

🦠
Biological — the most dangerous
Bacteria, viruses (Norovirus, Hepatitis A), moulds and their toxins. Source: unwashed hands, raw foods, infected food handlers, pests.
⚗️
Chemical — often invisible
Cleaning fluids, sanitisers, pesticides, food-grade lubricants. Prevent by: rinsing surfaces after disinfecting, storing chemicals away from food areas, using food-safe products only.
🔩
Physical — visible but serious
Glass, metal, bone, packaging, jewellery, hair, pest droppings. Causes choking and injury. Report broken equipment, wear hair nets, no jewellery in kitchen.
⚠️
Allergenic — life-threatening
Traces of allergens transferred to allergen-free food. Even microscopic amounts can trigger anaphylaxis. Covered in detail in Lesson 6.
🚫
Contamination Is Usually Invisible
Food that looks, smells and tastes fine can still be dangerously contaminated. Never rely on your senses alone.
Lesson 3 · Cross-Contamination

Colour-Coded Boards & Utensils

Cross-contamination is when harmful bacteria are transferred from one food or surface to another. The colour-coded system prevents this by keeping food types completely separate.

🔴 Red
Raw meat
🟡 Yellow
Raw poultry
🔵 Blue
Raw fish
🟢 Green
Salad/fruit/veg
🟤 Brown
Root veg/cooked
⬜ White
Bakery/dairy
🚿
Wash and sanitise boards between uses — even within the same colour
📦
Raw meat must be stored below ready-to-eat food in the fridge — always on the lowest shelf
🙌
Wash hands between handling raw and ready-to-eat foods — always, without exception
⚠️
Using a red board for vegetables after cutting raw chicken — even if it "looks clean" — is cross-contamination. It only takes invisible traces of bacteria to cause serious illness in a child.
Fridge Storage Order
Top shelf → ready-to-eat / dairy
Middle shelves → cooked food
Bottom shelf → raw meat/fish (in sealed containers)
Salad drawer → fruits and vegetables

Lesson 4 of 7

Cooking, Chilling & Stock Control

The 4 Cs of food safety are Contamination, Cooking, Chilling and Cleaning. This lesson covers cooking and chilling — the critical temperature controls that keep food safe.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~6 mins✅ 1 check
Lesson 4 · Safe Cooking Temperatures

Temperature Is Your Proof of Safety

Visual checks alone are not enough — food can look fully cooked while still harbouring dangerous bacteria in the centre. A probe thermometer is the only reliable check.

1
Core temperature must reach 75°C
Insert a clean, sanitised probe into the thickest part of the food. Hold for 10 seconds. 75°C destroys most food poisoning bacteria including Salmonella and E.coli.
2
Poultry — no pink, juices run clear
Both visual and temperature checks required for poultry. Campylobacter (the most common cause of food poisoning in the UK) lives in chicken and requires thorough cooking.
3
Reheating — 75°C minimum, once only
Food must reach 75°C when reheated. Food may only be reheated once — never reheat food that has already been reheated.
4
Record every temperature check
Log the food item, temperature achieved, time and your name. This is your legal HACCP record.
Probe Hygiene
Clean Probe = Accurate & Safe

A dirty probe can transfer bacteria directly into food. Always clean and sanitise the probe before use, between different foods, and after use. Use probe wipes or wash with hot soapy water then sanitise.

🌡️
Campylobacter — found in raw chicken — causes more food poisoning cases in the UK than any other bacteria. It is destroyed by reaching 75°C throughout.
Lesson 4 · Chilling, Cooling & Stock Rotation

Chill Fast, Store Smart

Incorrect cooling is one of the most common causes of food poisoning outbreaks. Hot food left to cool slowly passes through the danger zone for hours — giving bacteria time to multiply dangerously.

⏱️
Cool hot food to below 8°C within 90 minutes — divide into smaller portions, use shallow trays or ice baths to speed cooling
❄️
Never put large volumes of hot food directly into the fridge — it raises the fridge temperature, putting other food at risk
🏷️
FIFO — First In, First Out: Use older stock before newer. Label and date all opened/prepared food. Check use-by dates daily.
📦
Check all deliveries on arrival — reject anything past its use-by date, with damaged packaging, or delivered at the wrong temperature
🗑️
Food waste: Food waste bins must be lined, covered, emptied regularly and cleaned. Never allow food waste to accumulate near food preparation areas
📦
FIFO — First In, First Out
New stock goes behind old stock. Always use the oldest items first. Date every opened pack. Check the fridge every morning.

Lesson 5 of 7

Cleaning, Disinfecting & Pest Control

Clean and disinfect are not the same thing. Learn the critical difference, how to do both correctly, what a cleaning schedule requires, and how to spot and report pest activity.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~6 mins✅ 1 check
Lesson 5 · Clean vs Disinfect vs Sterilise

Three Different Things

These three terms are often confused but have very different meanings in food safety. Using the wrong process in the wrong area leaves surfaces contaminated even when they appear clean.

1
Cleaning — removes visible dirt and grease
Using detergent and water. Cleaning removes food residue and grease but does NOT kill bacteria. It is always the first step — you cannot disinfect a dirty surface effectively.
2
Disinfecting — kills bacteria to a safe level
Using a food-safe chemical disinfectant at the correct dilution and contact time. A surface must be clean first. Required on all food contact surfaces after cleaning.
3
Sterilising — kills all microorganisms
Used in very high-risk areas (e.g. baby bottle preparation). Typically achieved by heat, steam or chemical sterilant. Not routinely required in food preparation but essential for baby feeding equipment.
💡
The correct order is always: Clean → Rinse → Disinfect → Allow to dry. Disinfectant applied to a greasy surface is largely ineffective.
Contact Time Matters
Don't Wipe It Off Too Soon

Disinfectants need time to work. Always check the manufacturer's instructions for the required contact time — typically 30–60 seconds for spray disinfectants. Wiping immediately after spraying reduces effectiveness significantly.

⚠️
Never mix cleaning chemicals. Some combinations produce toxic gases. Always follow the manufacturer's instructions and use chemicals at the correct dilution.
Lesson 5 · Cleaning Schedules & Pest Control

Schedules & Pest Awareness

A cleaning schedule documents what must be cleaned, how often, by whom, and with what. It is a legal requirement under HACCP and will be inspected by EHOs.

📋
Every task on the cleaning schedule must be signed off when completed — incomplete records = non-compliance
🧽
Cloths and sponges harbour bacteria — use colour-coded, disposable cloths where possible. Never use the same cloth for food surfaces and floors
🚿
Dishcloths must be changed daily minimum, washed at 60°C, or single-use disposable
🐭 Rodents
Droppings, gnaw marks, greasy smear marks, nesting material, unusual smell
🪳 Cockroaches
Egg cases, black droppings, unpleasant smell, shed skins, live insects at night
🪰 Flies
Live/dead flies, maggots near waste, fly specks on surfaces, larvae in drains
🐜 Ants/Stored Product
Ant trails, webbing in dried goods, live insects in sealed packaging, unusual powder
🚨
Pest Sighting = Report Immediately
Any sign of pest activity must be reported to the manager the same day. Do not attempt to deal with it yourself. Food in the affected area must be quarantined until assessed.

Allergen Management

Lesson 6 · The 14 Major Allergens

UK Law: Declare All 14

Under UK food law (derived from EU Regulation 1169/2011), all food businesses must declare the presence of 14 major allergens. These must be highlighted on menus and available to parents on request.

🥜Peanuts
🌰Tree Nuts
🥛Milk
🥚Eggs
🌾Gluten/Wheat
🦐Crustaceans
🐟Fish
🦪Molluscs
🌱Soya
🌿Celery
🟡Mustard
🫘Lupin
🌻Sesame
🍷Sulphites/SO₂
Cross-Contact — Different From Cross-Contamination
Traces Are Enough to Kill

Cross-contact is when allergen traces transfer to allergen-free food. Unlike bacteria, allergens are NOT destroyed by cooking. A child with a peanut allergy can react to a board that was used to chop a peanut product — even if it was washed afterward.

📋
Every child's allergen profile must be checked before their first day and when the menu changes. This is the responsibility of every member of room staff.
Lesson 6 · Anaphylaxis Response

Recognise, Act, Call — In That Order

Anaphylaxis is a severe, potentially life-threatening allergic reaction. It can develop within minutes of exposure. Every member of staff must know the signs and the response steps.

1
Recognise the signs
Hives, flushing, swelling of lips/face/throat, difficulty breathing/swallowing, vomiting, pale, floppy or unconscious. Act on ANY suspected sign — do not wait.
2
Shout for manager and first aider
Do not leave the child alone. Call for help immediately — you need two people minimum.
3
Administer EpiPen per care plan
If the child has a prescribed adrenaline auto-injector, use it per their individual care plan. Know where it is stored before you ever need it — outer thigh, through clothing is acceptable in emergency.
4
Call 999 immediately
All anaphylactic reactions require emergency services — even after using the EpiPen. The EpiPen buys time but does not treat the reaction.
🚫
Never give antihistamines as a first response to suspected anaphylaxis. They act too slowly. Antihistamines treat mild reactions only — anaphylaxis requires adrenaline and 999.
📍
EpiPens and inhalers are NEVER locked away. They are kept accessible in the child's room at all times. Speed of access can be the difference between life and death.

Medication Management

Administering medication to children in our care is a serious legal and professional responsibility. Learn what can and cannot be given, the two-staff procedure, and safe storage and disposal.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~6 mins✅ 1 check
Lesson 7 · What We Can Administer

Rules Before Any Medication Is Given

Not all medication can be administered by nursery staff. Understanding these rules protects the child from harm and protects you from professional and legal liability.

✅
Prescribed medication — can be given with a completed, signed medication form and written parental consent
⚠️
Paracetamol / ibuprofen — only with GP prescription and written consent. Never administered "just in case" or to prevent fever
❌
First dose of antibiotics — must always be given at home first. We administer subsequent doses only with signed consent and completed medication form
❌
No verbal consent — a parent verbally saying "yes" at drop-off is never sufficient. The form must be signed before any medication is given
💉
EpiPens and inhalers — administered per individual child's care plan, regardless of consent form in an emergency
📝
No Form = No Medication
Without a signed medication form, no medication may be given. Contact the parent before the dose is due if no form is present.
Lesson 7 · The Two-Staff Procedure & Storage

Administering, Storing & Disposing Safely

Every dose administered at Early Explorers must follow a strict procedure. Two members of staff must always be involved — one to administer, one to witness.

1
Check the form is complete
Child name, medication name, dose, time, duration, parent signature — all must be present before proceeding.
2
Check the medication — witnessed
Second staff member confirms: correct name, correct dose, expiry date not passed. Both staff sign the record immediately after administration.
3
Storage: locked box or accessible
All medication → locked box in office. Exception: EpiPens and inhalers → always in the child's room, never locked away. Refrigerated medication → sealed labelled bag in fridge, separate from food.
4
Disposal: never in general waste
Unused/expired medication must be returned to parent or taken to a pharmacy. Record in medication log before disposal. Never pour liquids down the sink.
👥
Two staff members is non-negotiable. If a second member of staff is not available, the medication cannot be administered until one is. Contact the manager.
📋
Always inform the parent at collection. They must sign to acknowledge the medication was given. This closes the record and provides legal protection for all parties.
