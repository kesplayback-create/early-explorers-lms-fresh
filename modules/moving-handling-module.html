Why Manual Handling Matters

Understanding the importance of safe lifting protects both you and the children in your care. Let's explore the risks and why this training is essential.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 1 · The Reality
Manual handling injuries are the #1 workplace risk in childcare
Working in a nursery involves dozens of lifts every single day — from picking up babies, repositioning toddlers, to moving cots and equipment. Without correct technique, the cumulative strain causes serious, long-term injury.
73%
of nursery injuries are from manual handling
40+
lifts performed by a typical practitioner daily
#1
cause of long-term staff absence in early years
⚠️
Back injuries from incorrect lifting rarely resolve quickly. Many practitioners who ignore technique develop chronic conditions that end their careers in childcare.
🏥
The Hidden Cost
Each avoidable injury affects the whole team — covering shifts, managing stress, and impacting the quality of care children receive.
Did you know?
Your spine takes 8x your body weight when lifting incorrectly.
Lesson 1 · Who Is at Risk?
Everyone who lifts is at risk — but some more than others
👶
Baby room staff — lifting newborns from low cots multiple times per hour creates repeated spinal loading
🧒
Toddler room staff — toddlers are unpredictable movers; sudden shifts in weight are a common injury trigger
🏋️
Staff moving equipment — cots, chairs and sensory equipment are often heavy and awkward to manoeuvre
🆕
New staff — those without training are significantly more likely to injure themselves in their first 3 months
Legal Requirement
The Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 (as amended 2002)
Your employer must assess manual handling risks and provide training. You must follow that training and report any pain or concerns immediately.
Remember
No child or piece of equipment is worth a permanent injury.
If a situation feels unsafe — stop, assess, and ask for help.
Lesson 1 · Risk Assessment
Always assess using the TILE framework
Before any lift, mentally run through TILE. It takes only seconds and can prevent a serious injury.
T
Task — What does the lift involve?
How far are you moving? How long will you hold? Is the path clear?
I
Individual — Are you fit to lift?
Are you pregnant? Do you have back pain? Have you lifted a lot today?
L
Load — What are you lifting?
How heavy is the child? Are they likely to wriggle or reach suddenly?
E
Environment — Is the space safe?
Is the floor wet? Is there room to move? Is lighting adequate?
🧩
TILE in Practice
Think of TILE as your mental checklist. Over time it becomes instinctive — taking just 2–3 seconds before every lift.
✅
Practitioners who consistently use TILE reduce their injury risk by over 60%. Make it a habit from day one.

Safe Lifting Technique

The foundation of all manual handling. Master this technique and you protect your spine for life. Every lift should follow these principles.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 2 · The 6-Step Lift
The correct lift — step by step
Follow these 6 steps every time. They should become second nature within your first week.
1
Stop & assess
Run TILE. Is there a safer way? Do you need help?
2
Position your feet
Shoulder-width apart, one foot slightly forward for stability.
3
Bend your knees — not your back
Squat down to the child's level. Keep your back straight and natural.
4
Get close to the load
The closer the child is to your centre of gravity, the less strain on your back.
5
Lift smoothly using your legs
Drive upward through your legs. No jerking, twisting or rushing.
6
Move with control
Keep the child close. Move feet to change direction — never twist your spine.
🧍
Your Spine Under Load
A straight back distributes load evenly. A bent back concentrates enormous force on a few vertebrae — multiplied by the weight you're carrying.
⚠️
Never twist while holding a load. If you need to turn, move your feet first. Twisting under load is one of the most common causes of disc injury.
Lesson 2 · Do & Don't
Lifting: what to do and what never to do
✓ Do
✅ Keep your back straight and natural
✅ Bend at the knees
✅ Hold the child close to your body
✅ Move your feet to change direction
✅ Communicate with the child first
✅ Ask for help with heavier children
✅ Report pain immediately
✗ Don't
❌ Bend from the waist
❌ Twist your spine under load
❌ Hold the child away from your body
❌ Rush or jerk the movement
❌ Lift when you're tired or in pain
❌ Ignore discomfort or 'push through'
❌ Lift alone if unsafe to do so
💡
Print this Do/Don't list and display it in your staff room as a daily reminder until good technique becomes automatic.
⚖️
Correct vs. Incorrect
Correct technique takes the same amount of time as incorrect technique. There is no reason not to lift safely — every single time.

Lifting Babies & Toddlers

Young children present unique handling challenges. This lesson covers safe techniques specific to each age group in your care.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 3 · Babies (0–12 months)
Handling babies requires head & neck support at all times
👐
Always support the head — a baby's head is proportionally very large and neck muscles are not fully developed until around 4 months
🔔
Signal before you lift — speak softly and make eye contact before picking up a baby to avoid startling them, which causes sudden movement
⬆️
Lift from a kneeling or squatting position — never lean over a cot with your back bent; always lower the cot side and get close
🤝
Use both hands — one hand under the bottom for weight support, one cupped at the head and neck
💤
Adjust cot heights — always raise/lower cot sides fully before lifting. Reaching over raised sides is one of the biggest baby room injury risks
👶
Cot-to-Arm Transfer
Lower cot side → Kneel alongside → Slide hands under → Support head → Lift close to chest → Stand using legs → Settle baby against your shoulder
⚠️
Never lift a baby from a full-height cot without lowering the side first. The resulting reach-and-twist causes shoulder and back injuries.
Lesson 3 · Toddlers (1–3 years)
Toddlers are unpredictable — anticipate movement
Unlike babies, toddlers actively contribute to their own movement — and resist it too. This unpredictability is the primary injury risk in the toddler room.
🗣️
Always give a verbal warning — "Up we go!" before lifting. Toddlers who know what's coming don't lunge unexpectedly
🔒
Hold securely at the torso — place hands firmly on both sides of the torso, not just one side. Toddlers twist suddenly
📏
Keep them close immediately — toddlers may reach for objects while airborne. Bringing them in close limits their range of movement
⚖️
Know the weight limit guidance — if a toddler exceeds 15kg, consider whether a lift is necessary or a supported walk is safer
The Toddler Lunge Risk
A sudden lunge from a 14kg toddler generates over 30kg of momentary force through your spine.
The surprise, not just the weight, is what causes injury. Preparation and communication are your best protection.
🧒
Safe Toddler Lift
Verbal warning → Both hands on torso → Squat close → Smooth lift through legs → Hold close to chest → Watch for reaching
Lesson 3 · Nappy & Floor Work
High-repetition tasks: nappy changes & floor-level care
Nappy changes can happen 8–10 times per shift. The cumulative spinal load from poor posture adds up rapidly.
✓
Use a raised changing table
Always at waist height or above. Never change nappies on the floor if a table is available.
✓
Position yourself side-on
Stand alongside the change mat, not above it. This prevents the forward bend that strains your back.
✓
Floor play: use cushions or sit cross-legged
When floor-level care is necessary, sit rather than kneel or crouch to reduce prolonged spinal loading.
✓
Rotate positions regularly
Staying in one posture too long causes muscle fatigue. Change position at least every 20 minutes.
🔄
Posture Rotation
No single posture is perfect for extended periods. Varying between standing, sitting and kneeling keeps muscles active and spine healthy.
💚
Raising a change table by 10cm can reduce back muscle strain during nappy changes by up to 40%. Always adjust equipment to suit your height.

Supporting Children Safely

Not all handling is a lift — supporting, steadying, and guiding children also carries injury risk when done incorrectly.

📽 4 slides ⏱ ~5 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 4 · Types of Support
Supporting children involves more than just lifting
🚶
Walking support — holding a child's hand or arm while they walk. Avoid pulling upward on arms — risk of shoulder dislocation in under-3s
⬆️
Stair assistance — stand one step below the child, hand on their lower back or holding their hand at a comfortable height (not raised)
🍽️
Seating assistance — lowering a child into a high chair: hold at the torso, squat to lower, avoid leaning forward over the tray
😴
Settling to sleep — patting a baby in a cot: use a kneeling pad and kneel level with the cot rather than bending over the top
🤸
Nappy / dressing assistance — for mobile children, consider doing this standing at a wall bar or on a mat, reducing the need for repeated lifting
🤝
The "Nursemaid's Elbow" Risk
Pulling sharply upward on a young child's hand or forearm can dislocate the elbow (nursemaid's elbow). Hold at the wrist or upper arm, never yank upward.
⚠️
Never lift a child by their hands, wrists or under their arms if they are twisting or pulling away. The injury risk — to you and the child — is high.
Lesson 4 · Resisting Children
What to do when a child resists being lifted
A distressed, resisting child is one of the highest-risk lifting situations. Forcing a lift can injure both you and the child.
1
Pause — do not force the lift
Take a breath. A child actively resisting adds significant unpredictable force.
2
Communicate and distract
Use a toy, song or familiar routine to calm and engage before trying again.
3
Consider: is the lift necessary?
Can the child walk? Can you guide them safely without lifting? Choose the lowest-risk option.
4
Call for a colleague if needed
Two practitioners can assist far more safely than one struggling alone.
5
Document if the situation was hazardous
Log in the accident/near-miss book. This helps identify patterns and improve practice.
🆘
When to Say No to a Lift
There is no situation where completing a lift outweighs serious injury risk to you or the child. Pausing is always the right choice.
✅
Saying "I need help with this lift" is a sign of professionalism, not weakness. It protects children, colleagues, and yourself.

Moving Nursery Equipment

Cots, pushchairs, sensory equipment and furniture are a significant source of handling injuries. Learn how to move them safely.

📽 4 slides ⏱ ~5 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 5 · Equipment Handling
Key rules for moving nursery equipment safely
👀
Always check the path first — ensure the route is clear of children, wet floors, and obstacles before moving any equipment
⚖️
Know the weight before you lift — cots empty may be 20kg+; sensory equipment and storage units can exceed safe solo lift thresholds
👫
Two-person lifts for heavy items — never attempt to move a laden cot, heavy storage unit, or large sensory equipment alone
🛞
Use wheels and handles when available — push rather than carry where possible. Pushing uses far less spinal force than lifting
🔧
Report faulty or missing wheels/handles — a broken castor on a cot forces unsafe manual lifting. Report immediately and label as out of use
🛏️
The Empty Cot Problem
An empty nursery cot can weigh up to 25kg and is awkward to grip. Always use two people — one at each end — and communicate throughout the move.
25kg
Typical cot weight
2×
Always two people for cot moves
Lesson 5 · Pushchairs & Prams
Pushchairs and prams — a daily handling risk
Loaded pushchairs, especially twin buggies, are among the heaviest items moved daily in nurseries. Steps, ramps, and tight spaces increase risk significantly.
✓ Safe Practice
✅ Tilt back, not lift, for kerbs
✅ Use ramps wherever available
✅ Get help for steps with passengers
✅ Adjust handle height to your waist
✅ Communicate when moving in pairs
✗ Avoid
❌ Carrying a loaded pushchair
❌ Pushing with a bent-forward posture
❌ One-handed steering of heavy buggies
❌ Moving loaded prams up steps alone
❌ Using a pushchair as a storage trolley
🚼
Handle Height Matters
Pushing a pram with handles too low causes you to lean forward and round your back — multiplying spinal strain with every push. Always adjust handles to your waist height.

Preventing Staff Injury

The final lesson — putting it all together and ensuring you have the knowledge to protect yourself, your colleagues, and the children in your care every single day.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes 🏆 Module complete
Lesson 6 · Long-Term Health
Protecting your body is a long-term commitment
Most serious handling injuries are not caused by a single dramatic event — they are the result of repeated small stresses building up over months and years.
🔁
Every lift counts — cumulative strain from incorrect technique affects spinal discs gradually. The damage accumulates long before pain appears
🏋️
Core strength helps — simple core exercises (recommended by physiotherapists) can significantly reduce your injury risk over time
🧘
Stretch and recover — end-of-shift back stretches take 5 minutes and dramatically reduce next-day stiffness and long-term injury risk
🩺
Report early warning signs — stiffness, aching and mild pain are your body's early warning system. Do not ignore them or they will become serious
💚
Your Career, Your Health
Practitioners who follow correct technique and take care of their bodies work comfortably in childcare for decades. Those who don't often leave the profession early due to injury.
✅
You are the most important piece of equipment in this nursery. Protecting yourself is not optional — it is your professional responsibility.
Lesson 6 · Reporting
When and how to report pain or incidents
At Early Explorers, all staff have a responsibility to report handling incidents, near-misses and pain. This is not about blame — it is about improving safety for everyone.
1
Report any pain immediately to your manager
Do not wait until the end of the shift. Early reporting allows early intervention and prevents minor issues becoming serious.
2
Record in the accident/incident book
Date, time, what happened, what you were lifting, and any witnesses. Be factual and specific.
3
Report near-misses too
A near-miss today is an accident waiting to happen. Reporting them helps prevent future injury.
4
Follow any adjusted duties advice
If advised to avoid lifting after an injury, follow that advice fully — returning too early extends recovery time significantly.
RIDDOR
Certain manual handling injuries must be reported to the HSE under RIDDOR.
Your manager will handle this. Your role is to report promptly and accurately so they can do so.
📋
Document Everything
A well-documented accident book protects you, protects Early Explorers, and builds the evidence base for improving safety across the setting.
