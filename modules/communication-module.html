Why Partnerships Matter

Strong relationships with parents and carers are central to the EYFS framework. Learn why these partnerships are essential and what they mean in practice at Early Explorers.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~5 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 1 · The EYFS Partnership Principle

Parents Are Children's First Educators

The EYFS framework places strong emphasis on working with parents. They know their child better than anyone — our role is to work alongside them, not in place of them.

📋
Share observations and progress regularly — not just at formal reviews
🔑
Parents have the right to be involved in decisions about their child's care and learning
🌱
Two-way communication helps us spot changes in a child's wellbeing quickly
⭐
A child who sees their parent and key person communicating warmly feels more secure
👨‍👩‍👧
The Triangle of Care
Child · Parent · Practitioner — all three must communicate well for a child to thrive
📌
EYFS 2024 requires settings to build and maintain "respectful and trusting" relationships with all parents and carers
Lesson 1 · Daily Communication

Every Handover Matters

Drop-off and pick-up are your most important daily communication moments. Even a brief, warm, informative handover builds trust over time.

1
Greet by name — child and parent
Use both names. It signals you see them as individuals, not just another family.
2
Share one specific observation
"She painted a rainbow today and was so proud" means more than "she was fine".
3
Ask and listen
Has anything changed at home? Illness, a new sibling, a change in sleep? This affects behaviour and wellbeing.
4
Record important information
Verbal handover information must be recorded in the child's daily log same day.
Remember
Never Share Child Information at the Door

If you need to discuss something sensitive, ask the parent to step inside. Never discuss concerns, incidents or safeguarding matters in front of other families.

Difficult Conversations

Every practitioner will face a conversation with a parent that feels uncomfortable. Learn how to approach these professionally, calmly and constructively.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~5 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 2 · Approaching Difficult Conversations

Stay Calm, Stay Professional

Difficult conversations might include concerns about a child's development, a behaviour incident, or a parent complaint. Your approach matters as much as the content.

🏠
Always move to a private space — never discuss concerns at the front door or in earshot of other families or children
😌
Use calm, neutral language — avoid accusatory or defensive tone
👂
Listen fully before responding — let the parent finish speaking
📝
Always follow up difficult conversations in writing and inform your manager same day
🛑
When to Escalate Immediately
Any threat, aggressive behaviour, or safeguarding concern — stop the conversation and get your manager straight away
Lesson 2 · Handling Complaints

Complaints Are Opportunities

A complaint handled well can actually strengthen a parent's trust. A complaint handled poorly can end a relationship and damage the setting's reputation.

1
Acknowledge and thank them
"Thank you for raising this with us" — never be defensive. Their concern is valid even if you disagree.
2
Listen without interrupting
Take notes if needed. Don't make promises you can't keep.
3
Refer to the manager
Unless it's minor, formal complaints must be handled by the manager. Direct the parent accordingly.
4
Follow the complaints policy
All complaints must be logged and a written response provided within the timeframe set in our policy.
⚠️
Never discuss another child's behaviour with a parent as an explanation for an incident. This breaches confidentiality.
Key Principle
You Are Not Alone

If a conversation feels beyond your experience or becomes heated — stop and get your manager. You are never expected to handle serious complaints on your own.

SEND & Inclusive Communication

Communicating with parents of children with SEND requires additional sensitivity, consistency and collaboration. Learn what this looks like in practice.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~5 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 3 · Supporting SEND Families

Parents of SEND Children Are Partners in Planning

For children with identified or suspected SEND, parents must be actively involved in planning, reviewing and implementing support. This is a legal requirement under the SEND Code of Practice.

📋
Parents must be involved in creating and reviewing the child's SEND support plan
🗣️
Use accessible language — avoid jargon, acronyms or clinical terms without explanation
📞
More frequent, informal check-ins help build the trust needed for difficult conversations later
🤝
Always approach from a position of shared goals — you both want the best for the child
💙
The SENCO Is Your Ally
If a parent raises SEND concerns or you observe developmental differences, refer to our SENCO immediately. Don't wait.
Lesson 3 · Language & Barriers

Removing Communication Barriers

Not all parents communicate in the same way or in English as a first language. Our duty is to ensure every family can access information and feel heard.

1
Identify barriers early
During settling-in, note language needs, literacy levels, or communication preferences.
2
Use visual supports
Photos, visual daily diaries, and Makaton symbols help where language is a barrier.
3
Never use a child as an interpreter
This is inappropriate and puts unfair responsibility on the child. Contact the manager for support with translation needs.
4
Offer written summaries
Important information should be provided in writing so it can be translated or reviewed at home.
💡
The Equality Act 2010 requires us to make reasonable adjustments to ensure all parents can access our services equally — including communication.

Professional Boundaries & Confidentiality

Building warm relationships with families is important — but maintaining professional boundaries protects everyone. Learn where the lines are.

📽 3 slides⏱ ~5 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 4 · Professional Boundaries

Warm But Professional

Parents will sometimes want to become friends with staff. Warmth is encouraged — but personal friendships with families can create conflicts of interest and safeguarding risks.

📵
Do not accept personal friend requests from parents on social media while employed here
💬
Do not communicate with families via personal mobile numbers or messaging apps
🎁
Gifts must be declared to the manager — refer to the Staff Code of Conduct
🔒
Never share information about other children or families, even in casual conversation
⚖️
Why Boundaries Protect You
Blurred boundaries can lead to allegations, unfair treatment of other families, or safeguarding concerns about the staff member
Lesson 4 · Confidentiality in Practice

What Stays In the Room

Everything you learn about a child or family through your role is confidential. This includes development information, family circumstances, medical details and anything shared at handover.

1
Never discuss children outside work
Not to friends, family, or on social media — ever, even without using names.
2
Exceptions: safeguarding
Confidentiality is never a reason to withhold safeguarding concerns. The child's safety always comes first.
3
Data protection applies
Parent contact details, addresses and family information must not be shared or stored on personal devices.
⚠️
Breaching confidentiality is a serious disciplinary matter and may result in dismissal. When in doubt — say nothing and ask your manager.
