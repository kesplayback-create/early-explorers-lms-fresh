Your Role in Safeguarding

Every member of staff has a responsibility to protect children. This lesson refreshes your understanding of what safeguarding means and your duty in keeping children safe.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 1 · What is Safeguarding?
Safeguarding is everyone's responsibility
Safeguarding children means protecting them from maltreatment, preventing impairment of health and development, ensuring children grow up in safe and caring circumstances, and taking action to enable all children to have the best outcomes.
4
key areas of safeguarding
100%
of staff must understand their duty
24/7
safeguarding applies at all times
🛡️
Protecting from maltreatment — preventing abuse, neglect, domestic violence exposure
💚
Preventing impairment — ensuring access to healthcare, nutrition, safe environments
🌱
Ensuring safe circumstances — children grow up in environments that are caring and consistent
👁️
It Could Be You
You may be the only person who notices a sign of harm or hears a disclosure. Your vigilance can change — or even save — a child's life.
🚨
Critical: Safeguarding is not just about responding to abuse. It includes creating a culture where children feel safe, valued, and able to speak up.
Lesson 1 · Your Responsibilities
As a practitioner at Early Explorers, you must:
1
Know who the Designated Safeguarding Lead (DSL) is
At Early Explorers, this is [Manager Name]. Always report concerns directly to them.
2
Understand what constitutes abuse and neglect
Physical, emotional, sexual abuse, and neglect all require immediate action.
3
Recognize signs and indicators
Changes in behavior, unexplained injuries, disclosure — you must know what to look for.
4
Report concerns immediately — never delay
If you see something, say something. It is not your job to investigate — just to report.
5
Maintain confidentiality appropriately
Share information only on a need-to-know basis with the DSL and relevant authorities.
Legal Duty
Keeping Children Safe in Education (2023)
All staff have a legal duty to safeguard and promote the welfare of children. Failure to report concerns can result in criminal prosecution under the Children Act 2004.
⚖️
It Is Not Optional
Safeguarding is embedded in law. You have a professional and legal duty to act when you have concerns about a child's welfare.
Lesson 1 · What to Do & What NOT to Do
Clear guidance on responding to concerns
✓ Do
✅ Report concerns immediately to the DSL
✅ Listen carefully if a child discloses
✅ Write down exactly what was said
✅ Reassure the child they were right to tell
✅ Follow up to ensure action is taken
✅ Maintain professional boundaries at all times
✗ Never
❌ Promise confidentiality to a child
❌ Investigate concerns yourself
❌ Contact parents before speaking to DSL
❌ Ask leading questions
❌ Delay reporting because you're "not sure"
❌ Discuss concerns with colleagues unnecessarily
⚠️
If you are ever in doubt — report it. It is always better to have raised a concern that turns out to be nothing than to miss something that could have protected a child.
📝
Recording a Concern
Write: Date, time, what you saw/heard (use child's exact words if disclosure), your name. Sign and date. Pass to DSL immediately.

Types of Abuse & Neglect

Knowing what abuse looks like is essential to recognizing it. This lesson covers the four main categories and their indicators.

📽 6 slides ⏱ ~7 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 2 · Physical Abuse
Physical abuse: causing physical harm to a child
This includes hitting, shaking, throwing, poisoning, burning, scalding, drowning, suffocating, or otherwise causing physical harm. It also includes fabricated or induced illness.
🩹
Unexplained injuries — bruises, burns, fractures in unusual places or patterns
😨
Flinching or fear — child flinches when approached or shows fear of physical contact
📖
Inconsistent explanations — stories about injuries change or don't match the injury
👕
Covering up — wearing inappropriate clothing to hide injuries (e.g., long sleeves in summer)
🚨
Red Flag Injuries
Bruises to babies who are not yet mobile, injuries to ears or soft tissue areas, fingertip bruising, patterned injuries (e.g., belt marks) — all require immediate DSL reporting.
⚠️
Some injuries in very young children are almost never accidental. Any bruising to a non-mobile baby should be treated as a safeguarding concern.
Lesson 2 · Emotional Abuse
Emotional abuse: persistent emotional maltreatment
Conveying to children that they are worthless, unloved, inadequate, or valued only insofar as they meet another person's needs. It includes imposing age-inappropriate expectations, causing children to feel frightened or in danger, or exploiting or corrupting children.
😔
Low self-esteem — persistent feelings of worthlessness, being unloved or inadequate
👥
Attachment difficulties — struggles to form relationships, overly clingy or detached behavior
😠
Developmental delays — delayed speech, regression in milestones, age-inappropriate behavior
😨
Anxiety & fear — excessive worry, fearfulness around adults, frequent nightmares
💔
The Hidden Harm
Emotional abuse is often present alongside other types of abuse but can occur in isolation. It leaves no visible marks but causes profound long-term damage.
💡
Children who witness domestic abuse are experiencing emotional abuse — even if they are not directly harmed themselves.
Lesson 2 · Sexual Abuse
Sexual abuse: forcing or enticing a child to take part in sexual activities
This includes contact abuse (physical contact) and non-contact abuse (showing sexual images, grooming, online exploitation). Children cannot consent to sexual activity.
🔞
Inappropriate sexual knowledge — age-inappropriate understanding of sex or sexual language
😨
Behavioral changes — regression, nightmares, sudden fear of specific people or places
🩹
Physical signs — pain, bleeding, discharge, difficulty walking or sitting, recurrent UTIs
🚫
Sexualized behavior — inappropriate touching of self or others, drawing sexual images
🚨
Immediate Action Required
Any suspicion or disclosure of sexual abuse must be reported to the DSL immediately. Do NOT investigate. This is a police matter.
⚠️
Critical: Never ask a child to repeat a disclosure of sexual abuse. Write down exactly what they said the first time and report immediately.
Lesson 2 · Neglect
Neglect: persistent failure to meet a child's basic needs
Neglect is the most common form of abuse in the UK. It includes failure to provide adequate food, shelter, clothing, supervision, medical care, or emotional support.
🍽️
Persistent hunger — child is always hungry, hoards food, significant weight loss or failure to thrive
🧼
Poor hygiene — persistently dirty, smelly, inappropriate clothing for weather, severe nappy rash
🏥
Untreated medical needs — persistent nits, dental decay, untreated infections, missed immunizations
👁️
Lack of supervision — child left alone, wandering unsupervised, doesn't know where parents are
📊
Patterns Matter
One instance of poor hygiene doesn't mean neglect. But persistent, ongoing patterns — especially when support is offered but not acted on — are a safeguarding concern.
💡
Neglect can coexist with love. Parents may care deeply for their children but struggle with addiction, mental health, or poverty. Report concerns — families may need support, not judgment.

Recognizing Signs & Handling Disclosures

Children rarely tell adults directly that they're being harmed. Learn to recognize behavioral signs and how to respond when a child does disclose.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 3 · Behavioral Signs
Changes in behavior can indicate abuse
😔
Withdrawn or anxious — becoming quiet, isolated, fearful of making mistakes
😠
Aggressive outbursts — sudden anger, violence toward other children or adults
🍼
Regression — bedwetting, thumb-sucking, baby talk after being developmentally beyond it
😱
Fearfulness — excessive fear of specific people, reluctance to go home, fear of adults generally
🛌
Sleep issues — nightmares, trouble falling asleep, appearing tired constantly
🧩
Context Is Key
One sign alone rarely indicates abuse. Look for clusters of signs, sudden changes, or patterns over time. Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, report it.
💡
Children may test you before making a disclosure. They might say something small to see if you're safe to tell. Always take children seriously.
Lesson 3 · If a Child Discloses
How to respond when a child tells you about abuse
1
Stay calm and listen
Do not show shock, anger or disbelief. Your reaction can affect whether they continue.
2
Let them speak without interruption
Do NOT ask leading questions. Use: "What happened next?" or "Is there anything else?"
3
Reassure them
Tell them: "You were right to tell me. This is not your fault. I will help you."
4
Do NOT promise confidentiality
Explain you need to tell someone who can help, but only those who need to know.
5
Write it down immediately
Use the child's exact words. Include date, time, who was present, any visible signs.
6
Report to the DSL immediately
Same day. Do not delay, even if the DSL is not immediately available — escalate.
📝
Record Keeping
Your written record may be used in court. Be factual, use quotes, avoid opinion. Write "Child said: 'Dad hit me with a belt'" NOT "I think dad is violent."

Wider Safeguarding Concerns

Safeguarding extends beyond the four main abuse types. This lesson covers additional risks you must be aware of.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes 🏆 Module complete
Lesson 4 · Additional Risks
Safeguarding includes awareness of these specific harms
🏠
Domestic abuse — witnessing violence at home causes emotional harm and increases risk to the child
💊
Parental substance misuse — drugs/alcohol affecting parenting capacity and child safety
🧠
Parental mental health — when mental illness impacts ability to care for children safely
📱
Online safety — grooming, exploitation, inappropriate content, cyberbullying
✂️
FGM & honour-based abuse — culturally-motivated harms that are illegal and constitute abuse
🌍
Complex Families
Many families face multiple challenges. Your role is not to judge but to recognize when those challenges put children at risk, and to report.
⚠️
FGM is illegal and must be reported to the police. If you discover a girl under 18 has had FGM, or if you are told one is at risk, this is a mandatory reporting duty.
