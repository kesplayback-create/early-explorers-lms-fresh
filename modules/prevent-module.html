What is Prevent?

Understanding the Prevent duty, what radicalisation means, and why early years settings have a legal responsibility to protect children from extremism.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~7 minutes ⚖️ Legal duty
Lesson 1 · The Legal Duty
Prevent is a legal duty for all early years settings

The Counter-Terrorism and Security Act 2015 places a legal duty on all early years providers to have "due regard to the need to prevent people from being drawn into terrorism." This is known as the Prevent Duty.

2015
Act came into force
3+
Age groups covered — including early years
Part 5
Of KCSIE links Prevent to safeguarding
⚖️
Prevent is part of the government's CONTEST counter-terrorism strategy. It sits alongside Pursue, Protect and Prepare. As an early years setting, Prevent is embedded in our safeguarding responsibilities.
🏛️
Part of Safeguarding
Prevent is not separate from safeguarding — it is part of it. Protecting children from radicalisation is treated the same as protecting them from any other form of harm.
Lesson 1 · Key Definitions
Understanding radicalisation and extremism
🎯
Radicalisation — the process by which a person comes to support or engage in extremist ideologies or terrorism. It can happen gradually over time.
⚡
Extremism — vocal or active opposition to fundamental British values, including democracy, rule of law, individual liberty, and mutual respect.
🌐
Terrorism — the use or threat of action designed to influence government or intimidate the public for a political, religious or ideological cause.
👶
In early years — children are unlikely to become terrorists themselves, but may be exposed to extremist views within the family or community that affect their development.
⚠️
Radicalisation is a form of child abuse. A child being exposed to extremist ideologies is being harmed, even if there is no physical violence. Report concerns to your DSL.
🧠
It Can Affect Anyone
Radicalisation is not linked to any one religion, culture or community. Extremist ideologies exist across many different groups and belief systems.
Lesson 1 · British Values in EYFS
Promoting British Values at Early Explorers
The EYFS requires all early years providers to actively promote British values as part of the Prevent duty. These are not abstract concepts — they come alive through everyday interactions.
🗳️
Democracy
Children vote on activities, take turns leading, have their voices heard and respected
⚖️
Rule of Law
Clear consistent rules, understanding consequences, fairness and boundaries
🙋
Individual Liberty
Children make choices, express opinions, develop independence and confidence
🤝
Mutual Respect
Celebrating differences, kindness, inclusion — all children valued equally
💡
You promote British values every day through circle time, choice boards, celebrating different cultures and festivals, and modelling respectful behaviour. It does not require a separate lesson.

Signs & Indicators

What to look out for in children and families, what makes someone vulnerable to radicalisation, and what to do when you have a concern.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~7 minutes ✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 2 · Vulnerability
What makes someone vulnerable to radicalisation?
No single factor makes a child or family vulnerable. Radicalisation is complex and often involves a combination of personal, social and environmental factors.
💔
Identity crisis — struggling with personal identity, feeling excluded or marginalised from society
😞
Personal grievance — a sense of injustice, feeling that the world is against them or their community
👥
Social isolation — lack of positive social connections, susceptible to online grooming or extremist groups
🏠
Family influence — exposure to extremist views at home; children internalising attitudes they hear from adults
⚠️
Having these risk factors does not mean a child or family is radicalised. They indicate vulnerability and need for support — not suspicion. Always approach with sensitivity.
🤲
Support Not Suspicion
Prevent is about early intervention and support for vulnerable individuals — not about treating communities as suspects.
Lesson 2 · Indicators in Children
Signs to look out for in young children
Because of their age, signs in early years children are more likely to reflect exposure to extremist views at home rather than direct radicalisation of the child themselves.
💬
Language or drawings — using words or making drawings that reference violence, hatred of groups, or extreme ideologies unusual for their age
🚫
Exclusionary attitudes — expressing that certain groups of people are bad, wrong or deserve harm
😰
Fear or distress — anxiety linked to specific groups, events, or ideas that seems disproportionate or adult-influenced
🔄
Sudden changes — withdrawal, behavioural changes, or expressions of anger about political or religious topics
🚨
If a child makes a comment or drawing that concerns you, record the exact words or describe exactly what you saw, and report to the DSL immediately. Do not delay.
👂
Listen and Record
Write down exactly what the child said or did, as soon as possible after the incident. Use the child's own words. This is vital evidence for the DSL.
Lesson 2 · What to Do
If you have a Prevent concern
1
Record what you saw or heard
Write it down immediately — exact words, time, date, who was present. Do not rely on memory.
2
Report to your DSL straight away
Your Designated Safeguarding Lead handles all Prevent concerns. Do not try to manage this yourself.
3
Do not contact the family
Unlike some safeguarding concerns, you must not alert the family. The DSL will advise on next steps.
4
DSL may refer to Channel
Channel is a multi-agency safeguarding programme that provides support to vulnerable individuals. Your DSL manages this referral.
The Channel Programme
Voluntary, confidential support
Channel is not about criminalising individuals. It provides tailored support — mentoring, counselling, education — to help vulnerable people move away from extremist influence.
🤫
Prevent referrals are confidential. Do not discuss your concerns with colleagues unnecessarily — only with the DSL. This protects the family and the integrity of any investigation.

Your Responsibilities

Your specific duties under Prevent, how it links to safeguarding, and what Early Explorers does to meet the legal duty.

📽 5 slides ⏱ ~6 minutes 🏆 Module complete
Lesson 3 · Your Duties
What you are required to do
🎓
Complete Prevent training — you are doing this now. All staff must complete Prevent awareness training.
👁️
Remain vigilant — be aware of changes in children's behaviour, language, or drawings that may indicate concern
📋
Report concerns — any Prevent concern goes directly to the DSL, not to colleagues or parents
🌍
Promote British values — actively embed democracy, rule of law, liberty and mutual respect in daily practice
🔒
Online safety — ensure internet-enabled devices in the setting have appropriate filters and that children are not exposed to extremist content
🧑‍🏫
Every Member of Staff
Prevent is not just for managers. Every person working at Early Explorers has a personal legal duty to be aware and to report concerns to the DSL.
Lesson 3 · Prevent & Safeguarding
How Prevent links to our safeguarding duty
✓ Do
✅ Treat Prevent concerns the same as safeguarding concerns
✅ Record and report immediately to DSL
✅ Promote an inclusive, welcoming environment
✅ Challenge discriminatory language sensitively
✅ Celebrate diversity and different cultures
✗ Never
❌ Target or profile families based on religion or ethnicity
❌ Contact the family about a Prevent concern
❌ Dismiss concerns because they seem unlikely
❌ Discuss concerns with other staff unnecessarily
❌ Investigate yourself — refer to DSL
🚨
Do not contact the family when you have a Prevent concern. Unlike some safeguarding situations, alerting the family could compromise a referral or put others at risk.
