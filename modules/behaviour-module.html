Our Positive Behaviour Approach

At Early Explorers, we use positive, relationship-based strategies that support children's emotional development and self-regulation. This lesson explains our philosophy and why consistency matters.

📽 5 slides⏱ ~8 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 1 · Core Principles
Our behaviour approach is built on 5 core principles
💚
All behavior is communication — children express needs, feelings, and frustrations through behavior when they don't have the words
🤝
Relationships come first — strong, trusted relationships are the foundation of behavior support
🌟
Positive reinforcement over punishment — we focus on what children are doing well, not just what they're doing wrong
🧠
Understanding developmental stages — expectations must match children's developmental capabilities
🔁
Consistency across the team — every adult responds the same way so children feel secure and know boundaries
💬
Behavior IS Communication
A toddler hitting isn't "naughty" — they're communicating "I'm frustrated and I don't have the words yet." Our job is to teach the words.
💡
Young children's brains are still developing self-control. What looks like defiance is often developmentally normal — they literally cannot "just behave" yet.
Lesson 1 · What We DO at Early Explorers
Our strategies for positive behavior support
✓
Use "I notice..." and "I can see..."
"I notice you're building a tall tower!" Acknowledging effort builds connection and encourages positive behavior.
✓
Name and validate feelings
"You're feeling angry because Jack took your toy. It's okay to feel angry." Validation teaches emotional literacy.
✓
Offer choices
"Would you like the red cup or the blue cup?" Choice gives toddlers autonomy and reduces power struggles.
✓
Redirect and model alternatives
"Hitting hurts. Let's use gentle hands. Can you show me gentle?" Teach what TO do, not just what NOT to do.
✓
Use natural consequences
"The toys are on the floor. Let's tidy up together so we have space to play." Logical, immediate, related to the behavior.
🔑
The Power of Language
Say "walking feet" instead of "no running." Positive language tells children what to do, not just what to stop.
Lesson 1 · What We DON'T Do
Strategies we avoid at Early Explorers
❌
Shouting or raised voices — this frightens children and damages trust. Use a calm, firm voice always.
❌
Physical punishment — never smack, tap, or use any physical discipline. This is illegal and abusive.
❌
"Naughty chair" or isolation — young children cannot learn from exclusion. It causes shame without teaching alternatives.
❌
Labeling children — never call a child "naughty," "bad," or "difficult." This damages self-esteem and becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy.
❌
Threats or bribes — "If you don't stop, I'll..." teaches fear, not self-regulation. Natural consequences only.
⚠️
If you see a colleague using any of these approaches, speak to the manager immediately. These methods harm children and violate our safeguarding duty.
🚫
Zero Tolerance
Any form of punishment, humiliation, or harsh discipline is a safeguarding concern. If you witness it, you must report it.

Managing Challenging Moments

Tantrums, biting, and big emotions are developmentally normal. Learn practical strategies to support children through these moments calmly and effectively.

📽 5 slides⏱ ~8 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 2 · Tantrums & Meltdowns
Responding to tantrums at Early Explorers
1
Stay calm — you are the emotional anchor
Children mirror your emotions. If you stay calm, they recover faster.
2
Ensure safety first
Move them to a safe space if needed. Remove hard objects or other children from harm's reach.
3
Use minimal words during the peak
Don't try to reason or talk through it. Just stay close and safe. Save words for after.
4
Offer a calm presence
"I'm here. You're safe." Sit nearby but don't crowd them. Some children need space.
5
Name feelings AFTER the storm passes
"You were so upset when we had to leave the playground. That's hard." Then move on.
🌊
Meltdowns Are NOT Manipulation
Young children don't "throw tantrums for attention." Their nervous system is overwhelmed. They need co-regulation, not punishment.
💡
After the tantrum ends, move on. Don't lecture or bring it up repeatedly. Children feel shame afterwards — offer reassurance and reconnection.
Lesson 2 · Biting
Why toddlers bite and how to respond
Biting is common in toddler rooms and almost always stops by age 3. It is NOT a sign of aggression — it's a developmental phase driven by frustration, teething, sensory needs, or exploration.
1️⃣
Immediate response — Say firmly "Biting hurts. No biting." Comfort the child who was bitten first.
2️⃣
Redirect to alternatives — "If you need to bite, bite this teether. Not friends."
3️⃣
Investigate the trigger — Was the child overtired? Overstimulated? Frustrated? Record patterns.
4️⃣
Increase supervision — Shadow the child closely for the next few days to prevent and redirect before biting occurs.
🦷
Pattern Tracking
If a child bites repeatedly, track when/where/why. Most biting happens before nap time (tiredness) or during transitions (overstimulation).

Transitions & Routines

Most challenging behavior occurs during transitions. Learn how to structure the day and support smooth changes between activities.

📽 4 slides⏱ ~7 minutes✅ 1 knowledge check
Lesson 3 · Smooth Transitions
Strategies to support transitions
⏰
Give 5-minute and 2-minute warnings — "In 5 minutes we'll be tidying up." Prepares children for change.
🎵
Use songs and visual timers — "Clean-up song" signals transition. Visual sand timers help children see time passing.
🤝
Transition together — Don't send children alone. Move as a group with an adult leading.
🧩
Offer a job or role — "Can you be my special helper and carry the basket?" Gives purpose during the transition.
🎯
Name what's coming next — "First we'll wash hands, then snack time!" Predictability reduces anxiety.
⏱️
Predictability = Security
Young children thrive on routine. Knowing what's coming next helps them feel safe and reduces behavioral challenges.
💡
The most challenging transition times: arriving/leaving, before meals, before nap. Expect more support needed during these windows.

Consistency & Teamwork

Children need all adults to respond the same way. This final lesson covers how we work as a team to support positive behavior across the setting.

📽 4 slides⏱ ~7 minutes🏆 Module complete
Lesson 4 · Why Consistency Matters
Consistent responses help children feel secure
When every adult responds the same way, children learn boundaries faster, feel safer, and show fewer behavioral challenges. Mixed messages cause confusion and anxiety.
✓
Same language across the team — "Gentle hands," "walking feet," "kind words" — everyone uses the same phrases
✓
Same boundaries — If one practitioner allows something, all practitioners should allow it
✓
Same consequences — Natural consequences applied calmly and consistently by everyone
💬
If you're unsure how to respond to a behavior, ask a colleague or your room leader. We support each other to stay consistent.
🧩
One Team, One Approach
Children should experience the same calm, positive responses from every adult in the setting — no matter which room or which shift.
